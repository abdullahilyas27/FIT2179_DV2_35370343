Beneath the Surface

Why the headline about shark attacks is wrong Australian shark incidents · 1791–2026 By Abdullah Ilyas Siddiqui · ID 35370343

Every summer the same sentence returns: shark attacks are on the rise. Counted one way, it is true — Australia records more bites now than at any point in two centuries of keeping track. But a count is not a risk, and the rest of this page is an argument with that headline.

Across 1,296 recorded incidents we follow the numbers down through fourteen charts. The raw tally climbs; the chance that you are bitten falls. The story turns out to be about us — more people, in warmer water, doing more in it — and far less about the sharks.

Reading the line

Three moments anchor the climb — but climbing what? Raw counts cannot say whether Australians face more dangerous beach days or simply more beach days. The next chart draws the distinction, and it changes everything.

The headline writes itself: more bites every decade. But the population doubled, then doubled again. Per person, the very same line tells the opposite story.

04 / 14 · The inversion

Incidents rose. The risk to each of us did not.

The headline number climbs; the rate beneath it does the opposite. Population multiplied, recorded incidents rose in absolute terms — but per-capita risk per million residents has gradually fallen since the 1930s.

Between the 1910s and 2010s incidents grew about 4.3-fold; population grew roughly five-fold. Divide one by the other and the alarming line bends quietly toward the floor.

What this means

If risk per person is falling, something downstream must be growing faster than the rate declines. That gap is the rest of this investigation. Two forces emerge.

05 / 14 · Force one · activity

Boards changed everything.

Swimming dominated mid-century exposure. Surfing and boarding took over after 1980, tracking the rise of board culture along the east coast and putting far more bodies past the break.

Each new wave of water sport creates a new front of exposure: swimmers in the 1920s, boarders in the 1980s, divers and spearfishers since.

The headline lies again

Tigers and bulls aren't merely present — they're disproportionately deadly per encounter. White sharks lead the absolute fatal count because we meet them so often, not because each meeting is the most lethal. Next: what were we doing when bitten?

A moving picture

The story is not static. Early-century reports were tiger-heavy with a fat "Other" category — identification was rough and the database caught what it could. Modern decades show cleaner attribution and the absolute rise of white-shark incidents that headlines now fixate on.

Numbers can hide what numbers describe. Each red mark on the chart that follows is a single life lost — not a percentage, not a rate. Every one.

Holding both truths

More dots each decade, a lower curve beneath them. The risk to any one person has fallen even as the toll, in absolute lives, has not. Both are real.

Conclusion

What 236 years actually say.

Recorded shark incidents in Australia are rising. So is the population. So is participation in surfing and diving. So is sea-surface temperature in every region we measure. Per-capita risk, meanwhile, is gently falling — and fatality rates have fallen faster as trauma medicine improved across the twentieth century.

The bite count is a story about us being in the water more, not about sharks being in the water more. The fear that something is getting worse is real; the data, read carefully, says otherwise. That is the whole argument of this page — set against a single, stubborn headline.
